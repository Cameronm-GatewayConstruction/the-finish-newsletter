Market Overview

Tenant improvement construction is its own ecosystem — & the only one we operate in. Ground-up development, large-scale commercial new builds, infrastructure — that's not our world. Our world is commercial interiors, exclusively, across the Seattle Metro Area. And after years of building in this specific market, we've learned to read it closely.

The first quarter of 2026 gave us a lot to read.

In this issue, we're sharing what we observed on the ground this quarter — from the continued spec suite surge and what TI dollars are actually delivering right now, to construction input costs that have risen steadily but show signs of stabilizing, to why the most successful projects we saw this quarter had one thing in common: the GC was at the table early.

This is a builder's report — written for the people who design, lease, own, and occupy the commercial interiors of the Puget Sound.

The 'Spec Suite Boom' — Data & Insights

Downtown Bellevue vacancy sat at 25.4% at year-end, while downtown Seattle hit a record high of 34.7% according to CBRE. Generally speaking, high vacancy environments lead to landlords competing strongly for tenants, which leads to the use of spec suites as a competitive differentiator.

It's worth noting that, according to Newmark, most demand in the region (particularly Seattle) is still centered around A+ product in A+ locations. Conversely, Newmark suggests that Class-B product offers historically low rents and historically high concessions — but still lose out to higher-quality assets. This is critical in understanding why landlords are building spec suites rather than just lowering rents.

To further corroborate this, Kidder Matthews' Q4 2025 Seattle Office Market Report noted "a clear flight to quality remains evident, with tenants prioritizing Class-A and amenity rich buildings in prime locations."

Spec Suite Dynamics — What's Happening

Spec suites have become a 'go-to' strategy with many of the region's landlords — who are building them because tenants don't want to go through the construction process, especially companies downsizing amid uncertainty about office utilization.

Particularly those under 15,000 SF — these tenants move with urgency once they've made up their minds. When a space is raw, design, permitting and buildout can easily outrun a tenant's patience. Spec suites close that gap.

With turnkey spec suites, landlords cover the entire build-out cost upfront rather than sharing it through a TI allowance — some owners view it as a sunk cost, and are often open to shorter term leases of 3–5 years in exchange, according to Newmark.

The Design Evolution of Spec Suites

Historically, spec suites tended to be vanilla and relatively inexpensive — heavy on paint and carpet. Today, a lot more is needed to create a compelling space that supports the flight to quality. Tenants want high-performance spaces that attract employees and boost recruitment.

JLL's 2025 design outlook notes that high-performance environments now require spaces that go beyond material aesthetics, with greater focus on sensory experience, acoustics, and lighting — features with proven physiological and psychological benefits tied to productivity and wellbeing.

Construction Dive (citing JLL) claims that "Hospitality-style design, premium finishes, high-end amenities and agile workspaces have become baseline expectations of today's workforce — with offices now expected to embody brand, culture and adaptability."

So, with an 80% buildout, the remaining 20% of construction can incorporate tenant's brand & persona in just weeks — making spec suites a powerful hybrid between move-in-ready and customizable.

The Gateway Construction Angle

The GC & Design takeaway here is that spec suite projects are (most often) no longer simple or cheap anymore. What we've seen in the majority of our projects in the latter half of 2025 and the start of this year is that landlords who are competing for quality tenants are investing real money into real finishes.

They need experienced TI contractors to execute on tight timelines without sacrificing quality. Speed & quality are no longer competing priorities — the market is demanding both simultaneously.

Second-Generation Spaces

Per the previous sections of this report, there's no shortage of data confirming that demand for Class-A office space in the Seattle/Bellevue region remains strong. But here's a distinction worth making — Class A demand and first-generation Class A demand are not one & the same. And from where we sit, that difference is becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.

We cannot speak for every TI contractor in the market. But internally, we've noticed a meaningful uptick in invitations to bid on second-generation spaces — previously occupied, quality buildings where a new tenant is ready to invest in a serious build-out rather than wait for new product or settle on raw space.

When OpenAI committed to a 296,000 SF footprint at City Center Plaza in Bellevue — adding approximately 10 additional floors to their existing presence in the building — they weren't moving into freshly delivered product. They were moving into a building previously occupied entirely by Microsoft — a second-generation space that required a full, high-end tenant improvement build.

The scope included an interconnecting staircase, a 140-seat cafeteria, a wellness center, tech bar, collaborative areas, and more. Second-gen space, first-class results.

Snowflake did the same thing — securing their Bellevue office through a sublease at Block 6 in the Spring District, a space Meta had vacated before ever occupying. Again, a marquee tenant choosing an existing building over brand new product.

The pattern is hard to ignore. Demand for quality in this market is real and well-documented — but quality and new construction are not always the same thing. Some of the most significant leases in Q1 2026 were tenants choosing well-located, previously occupied Class-A buildings and investing in the TI work needed to make those spaces their own.

The Constructo-nomics

First-generation buildouts are generally more expensive on average, in our experience — 2nd gen space can reduce that baseline meaningfully by leveraging existing infrastructure. Mechanical systems, electrical distribution, plumbing rough-ins — when they're already in place and in serviceable condition, the cost and schedule advantages are real. In a market where TI allowances are being negotiated hard and every dollar of construction budget is scrutinized, that head start matters.

Part of it is timing. A second-gen space with good bones can often be delivered faster than a raw first-gen build — and in a market where tenants are moving quickly once they commit, schedule is currency.

And part of it is simply availability. With first-gen trophy product like Four106 in Bellevue looking to be absorbed rapidly by the likes of two confidential anchor tenants, mid-market and even enterprise tenants are finding that the best available locations aren't always the newest ones.

The opportunity in second-gen space is genuine. So is the importance of knowing what you're walking into before you commit.

Old bones can absolutely support a new build. You just need to know whose hands are doing the work.

The Gateway Construction Angle
There's an honest caveat — and it's an important one.

Second-generation TI work is not without risk. What looks like a cost-efficient starting point on paper can change quickly once you open the walls. Outdated MEP systems, unforeseen structural conditions, hazardous materials, undersized electrical infrastructure — these are real variables that can erode projected savings fast. Industry guidance suggests carrying contingency allowances of 5–10% or higher on second-gen projects for exactly this reason.

This is where experienced TI contractors earn their keep. At Gateway, we've opened enough walls in enough buildings across the region to know that due diligence on existing conditions isn't optional — it's the difference between a project that delivers on its promise and one that doesn't.

Key Takeaways
  • Gateway notices a slightly larger ratio of second-gen TI RFPs to first-gen RFPs compared to previous quarters, despite the surplus of first-generation trophy space in the region
  • There is no objective proof that second-generation space is in higher demand than new construction offices — but data supports it can be both cheaper and quicker than a cold-shell TI
  • When considering a second-gen project, cost savings are real — but so is the risk. Unforeseen conditions are a legitimate threat to viability; engage contractors early to understand and mitigate

The broadest measure of construction cost pressure rose just 0.1% quarter-over-quarter in Q1 2026 — essentially flat, and a reassuring signal for TI budgets heading into the year. The full picture since January 2025 tells a similarly calm story: cumulative growth of just 1.5% over 15 months. While individual inputs are moving in different directions, the overall basket remains stable.
Copper & Brass
+3.4%
2026 Q1
Copper is the standout mover of Q1 2026 — up 3.4% quarter-over-quarter and now 10.7% above its January 2025 baseline. After a brief plateau in mid-2025, copper resumed its climb and shows no signs of slowing. Critical for electrical wiring and plumbing rough-ins, projects with heavy MEP scope should anticipate continued pressure, particularly given ongoing tariff exposure on imported copper.
Steel Mill Products
+2.9%
2026 Q1
Steel has been the most dramatic mover of the entire period — climbing steadily from its January 2025 baseline to reach 111.8 by March 2026, an 11.8% increase. The Q1 2026 quarter-over-quarter gain of 2.9% continues a consistent upward trend that began in mid-2025. Metal stud framing and hollow metal doors — staples of TI work — are directly exposed to this pressure.
Lumber & Plywood
+1.3%
2026 Q1
Lumber was the most stable input through most of 2025, holding flat at its January 2025 baseline before ticking upward in late Q3. By Q1 2026 it sits at 102.1 — a modest 2.1% cumulative gain. Used for blocking, backing, and casework substrate in TI work, lumber remains one of the calmer inputs in the basket, though Canadian tariff exposure bears watching.
Gypsum Products
+0.2%
2026 Q1
Drywall costs have climbed quietly and consistently throughout the entire period — never a dramatic spike, but never a pause either. At 102.8 by March 2026, gypsum is up 2.8% since January 2025, though Q1 2026 itself was nearly flat at +0.2%. Because drywall is present in virtually every TI project we build, this slow and steady upward drift compounds across project budgets over time more than any single line item suggests.

H2 2026  ·  Seattle Metro
Forecast 01
Amenities Keep Winning
The flight to quality isn't slowing — it's broadening. What started as demand for better workstations and finishes has expanded into a full rethinking of the office environment. Landlords across Seattle and Bellevue are investing not just in spec suites, but in lobbies, corridors, fitness centers, conferencing facilities, and shared amenity floors. The underlying logic is the same: in a market where tenants have options, the building experience matters. We expect amenitization to remain one of the most active drivers of TI and capital improvement work through the remainder of 2026 and into 2027.
Forecast 02
Second-Generation TI Activity May Continue to Grow
Gateway has observed a gradually increasing share of second-generation TI invitations relative to first-generation cold-shell work — and market data suggests this trend has structural support. New office construction has effectively stalled: speculative office construction in the Puget Sound region declined 95% year-over-year by end of 2025, with under 64,000 SF remaining under construction across the entire market. On the Eastside, no new office buildings are expected to be delivered in 2026 — the first time in a decade — with activity instead centered on existing buildings where over 2.3M SF is being built out. With the pipeline of brand-new first-generation space drying up, tenants seeking quality space in established buildings will increasingly encounter second-generation opportunities. Whether that translates into a formal preference remains to be seen, but the conditions favor it.
Sources: Kidder Mathews Q1 2026 Seattle Office Market Report  ·  Broderick Group Q1 2026 Eastside Market Report
Forecast 03
Construction Costs — Elevated, But Stabilizing
Gateway's Q1 2026 cost index showed continued upward pressure across key input categories, though the pace of increases has moderated compared to prior years. We're cautious about predicting meaningful relief in the near term — labor markets remain tight and material costs have not meaningfully retreated. What the data does suggest is that the extreme volatility of 2022–2024 may be behind us. A more predictable cost environment, even at elevated levels, is constructive for project planning. Teams that engage contractors and begin pre-construction work early will be better positioned to hold budgets than those entering the market cold.
Source: Gateway Construction Services — Internal Project & Bid Activity, Q4 2025 – Q1 2026
